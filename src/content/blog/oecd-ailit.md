This month, the OECD and European Commission published the final version of the **AILit Framework: Empowering Learners for the Age of AI**. It is the most significant international education policy document on AI since UNESCO's 2023 guidance — and it carries a deadline attached.

**PISA 2029 will assess AI and Media literacy for the first time.** That gives education systems roughly three years to get this right.

Here is what school leaders and policymakers need to understand about this framework — and why the urgency is real.

---

## AI Literacy Is Not What Most Schools Think It Is

The framework opens with a distinction that challenges most current practice: **AI literacy is different from AI tool use.**

This matters enormously. A student who can navigate ChatGPT fluently is not necessarily AI literate. A school that has rolled out an AI writing assistant has not necessarily built AI literacy. Tool familiarity and genuine literacy are not the same thing — and conflating them is the most common mistake I see schools making right now.

The framework defines AI literacy as *the technical knowledge, durable skills, and future-ready attitudes that allow learners to understand how AI systems work, critically evaluate their outputs, and use them ethically and creatively.*

Note what is foregrounded: **understanding, criticality, ethics.** Not efficiency. Not productivity. Understanding.

---

## Four Domains Every School Leader Should Know

The AILit Framework organises AI literacy into four domains:

### 1. Engage with AI
Understanding what AI is, how it works, and recognising it in daily life — including when it operates invisibly.

### 2. Create with AI
Using AI as a collaborator to produce, design, and generate in ways that are purposeful and original.

### 3. Manage AI
Making responsible decisions about when, whether, and how to use AI — including privacy, data, and bias considerations.

### 4. Shape AI
Understanding that AI systems reflect human choices, and that learners have agency to influence how AI is designed and governed.

That fourth domain is the one I find most significant. It positions students **not as consumers of AI, but as future architects of it.** That is a fundamentally different educational ambition — and it has profound implications for curriculum design.

## What This Means for School Leaders Right Now

This framework is non-binding. No country is legally required to implement it. But the PISA 2029 connection changes the stakes considerably. Education systems that ignore AI literacy now will face uncomfortable comparisons in four years.

More immediately, the framework offers something school leaders have been asking for: **a shared language.** One of the biggest barriers to AI literacy implementation has been the absence of a common definition — every school, district, and ministry has been improvising. The AILit Framework ends that excuse.

### Three practical steps for school leaders:

**First, read the framework.** It is 60 pages, free, and written in accessible language. It is available at [ailiteracyframework.org](https://ailiteracyframework.org) — not buried behind a paywall.

**Second, audit your current provision.** Ask honestly: are we teaching students to *use* AI tools, or to *think critically* about AI systems? The answer will tell you where your gaps are.

**Third, start with teachers.** The framework is explicit that educators are central to this vision. AI literacy cannot be outsourced to a digital skills class or a one-off assembly. It needs to live across subjects — in science, in civics, in language arts. That requires teacher professional development, not just student programs.

---

## A Final Thought

The OECD's framing of this as a **citizenship challenge, not a technology challenge,** is exactly right. The question of whether our students can think critically about AI is the same question as whether they can participate meaningfully in democratic society over the next 30 years.

School leaders who treat this as an IT department problem will find themselves in the same position as those who treated media literacy as optional in the early social media era — and we know how that turned out.

**The framework is here. The timeline is set. The question now is simply: what are we going to do with it?**
